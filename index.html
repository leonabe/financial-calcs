Simple and compound interest

Simple interest

Interest is calculated only on the original principal. The balance grows in a straight line.

A = P(1 + rt)
I = Prt

For £1,000 at 5% for 3 years, simple interest gives £1,150.

linear growth no interest-on-interest quick approximations

Compound interest

Interest is calculated on the growing balance. Interest itself starts earning interest.

FV = PV(1 + r)t
FV = PV(1 + r/n)nt

For £1,000 at 5% for 3 years, annual compounding gives about £1,157.63.

curved growth interest-on-interest investing engine

Nominal and effective rates

The difference

A nominal rate is the quoted annual rate. The effective annual rate is what actually happens after compounding.

EAR = (1 + r/n)n − 1

More frequent compounding increases the effective rate.

Example

12% nominal compounded monthly:

EAR = (1 + 0.12/12)12 − 1 ≈ 12.68%

The headline says 12%, but the actual annual rate is about 12.68%.

Future value and present value

Future value

Future value moves money forward through time.

FV = PV(1 + r)t

It asks: what does money today become later?

Present value

Present value discounts money backward through time.

PV = FV / (1 + r)t

It asks: what is future money worth today?

Discount factors

Concept

A discount factor tells us what £1 received in the future is worth today.

DF = 1 / (1 + r)t
PV = FV × DF

If the discount factor is 0.8638, then £1 in the future is worth about £0.8638 today.

Interpolation and extrapolation

Concept

Interpolation estimates a value between known data points. Extrapolation estimates outside the known range.

y = y₁ + [(x − x₁)/(x₂ − x₁)](y₂ − y₁)

Interpolation is usually safer. Extrapolation assumes the pattern continues beyond the known data.
